Energy Bands in Materials
In solid materials, electrons exist in specific energy ranges called energy bands. The Valence Band is
the lower energy band where electrons are tightly bound to atoms and cannot move freely. The
Conduction Band is the higher energy band where electrons can move freely and conduct electricity.
The Forbidden Energy Gap is the energy difference between the valence band and the conduction
band. Conductors have no energy gap, semiconductors have a small energy gap, and insulators have a
large energy gap.
Conductors, Semiconductors, and Insulators
Conductors such as copper and aluminum allow electricity to flow easily because their conduction and
valence bands overlap. Semiconductors such as silicon and germanium have a small forbidden energy
gap, which allows controlled conductivity. Insulators such as rubber and glass have a large energy gap,
preventing the flow of electrons.
Intrinsic Semiconductors
An intrinsic semiconductor is a pure semiconductor material without impurities. At absolute zero
temperature, it behaves like an insulator. As temperature increases, electrons gain energy and move to
the conduction band, leaving behind holes in the valence band. In intrinsic semiconductors, the number
of electrons is equal to the number of holes.
Extrinsic Semiconductors
Extrinsic semiconductors are formed by adding impurities to intrinsic semiconductors to increase
conductivity. This process is called doping. Depending on the impurity added, semiconductors become
N-type or P-type.
N-Type Semiconductor
N-type semiconductors are created by adding pentavalent impurities such as phosphorus, arsenic, or
antimony. These impurities provide extra free electrons. In N-type materials, electrons are the majority
carriers and holes are the minority carriers.
P-Type Semiconductor
P-type semiconductors are formed by adding trivalent impurities such as boron or aluminum. These
impurities create holes in the valence band. In P-type materials, holes are the majority carriers and
electrons are the minority carriers.
Charge Carriers
Charge carriers in semiconductors include electrons and holes. Electrons carry negative charge and
move in the conduction band, while holes carry positive charge and move in the valence band. Both
contribute to electric current.
Drift Current
Drift current occurs when an external electric field is applied to a semiconductor. Electrons move
toward the positive terminal, while holes move toward the negative terminal. This movement creates
electric current proportional to the electric field strength.
Diffusion Current
Diffusion current occurs due to the concentration gradient of charge carriers. Charge carriers move
from regions of high concentration to low concentration without external electric fields.
Hall Effect
The Hall Effect is observed when a current carrying semiconductor is placed in a magnetic field
perpendicular to the current. A voltage is developed across the material. This effect helps determine
carrier concentration, mobility, and the type of semiconductor.
Einstein Relation
The Einstein relation connects the diffusion coefficient and mobility of charge carriers in
semiconductors. It shows that both parameters are directly proportional to temperature.
Mass Action Law
The Mass Action Law states that the product of electron concentration and hole concentration in a
semiconductor remains constant at thermal equilibrium. In intrinsic semiconductors, electron
concentration equals hole concentration.
Applications of Semiconductors
Semiconductors are used in diodes, transistors, solar cells, integrated circuits, LEDs, sensors, and
communication devices. They are fundamental components in modern electronics including computers,
smartphones, and satellites.
